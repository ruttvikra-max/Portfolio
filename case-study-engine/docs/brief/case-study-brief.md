# Rules Engine — Portfolio Case Study Brief

> Extracted from "Portfolio Case Study Guidelines.docx". This is the source of truth for tone, structure, and content. All drafts must follow it.

---

## Role & Voice
Write as a **Design Director** at a top product company (Stripe, Linear, Figma, Notion, Airbnb, Atlassian, Microsoft) with a very high storytelling bar.

- This is **NOT a UX case study** — it's a **portfolio story** meant to convince another Design Director to interview the designer.
- Writing should feel **editorial, thoughtful, concise** — not academic.

## Objective
A premium portfolio case study for an **enterprise Rules Engine** that communicates how the designer thinks.

Goal is **not** to show every screen. Demonstrate:
Product thinking · Business understanding · Systems thinking · Enterprise UX · Decision making · Collaboration · Design maturity.

By the end, recruiters should think: *"This designer understands business problems, influences product direction, collaborates with engineering, and simplifies highly technical systems into intuitive experiences."*

## Writing Principles
- Write like Stripe / Linear / Notion — not a UX bootcamp.
- Avoid generic UX language, obvious design concepts, filler.
- Avoid: "We conducted research…", "We created personas…", "Then we wireframed…", "Then we designed…"
- Focus on: **WHY** decisions were made · business reasoning · tradeoffs · constraints · product strategy.

## Length & Tone
- Readable in **5–7 minutes**, ~**1,500–2,000 words**.
- Every section must earn its place; every paragraph delivers new info.
- Tone: confident, professional, product-focused, enterprise-focused. Never exaggerate, never marketing copy, never sound like AI. Short paragraphs, impactful headlines.

## The Project
- **Project:** Enterprise Rules Engine for US Healthcare organizations.
- **Status:** Currently in development.
- **Timeline:** 6 months.
- **Role:** Lead Product Designer.
- **Ownership:** Entire product experience from discovery to high-fidelity designs, while actively contributing to product strategy.
- **Team:** Product Manager · Engineers · Data Scientist · Designer (me).

## Business Problem (before the product)
- Rule configuration was manual.
- Every rule change required engineering support.
- Launching a new healthcare market took nearly **three weeks**.
- Multiple support tickets due to a fragmented workflow.
- Rule management wasn't scalable.

Business need: a platform letting **operations teams configure business rules independently of developers**. Larger outcome: not "another internal tool" but **reducing operational dependency while improving scalability, governance, and client confidence.**

## My Contributions (weave into the story, not a bullet list)
Prioritizing features · defining product workflows · challenging engineering decisions · pushing back on stakeholders when complexity wasn't solving user problems · validating product ideas · conducting product demos.

## The Big Product Insight (make this a strong moment)
Stakeholders focused on advanced capabilities. Instead of "What more can we build?", I asked **"Why are users still relying on Excel?"**

Answer: users didn't want more functionality — they wanted **confidence, familiar workflows, speed.** They wanted something **easier than Excel, not more powerful than Excel.** This became a core design principle.

## Core Product Features (group into workflows, don't explain each)
Rule Builder · Rule Library · Rule Validation · Rule Testing · Version History · Audit Trail · Rule Dependencies · Search · Filters · Tags · Source Citation · Rule Duplication · Bulk Editing · AI Assistant · Draft Mode · Permissions · Rule Preview · Visual Flow Builder.

## Major UX Challenges
Designing for: thousands of rules · information density · searchability · dependencies · rule validation · source citation · rule testing · user permissions · non-technical healthcare users.
Audience is Excel-heavy. Interface must feel approachable without sacrificing enterprise power.

## Major Design Decision
Pushed back against unnecessary complexity. Advocated a simpler workflow that solves today's problems first instead of exposing every advanced capability upfront. Reduced cognitive load, improved usability, accelerated rule creation. Demonstrates product thinking over interface design.

## Business Impact (meaningful outcomes, not inflated metrics)
- Reduce market launch time from ~3 weeks to ~3 days.
- Reduce engineering dependency.
- Improve governance.
- Improve discoverability.
- Reduce configuration errors.
- Make audits easier.
- Build greater client trust.

## Case Study Structure (ONLY these sections — no others)

1. **Hero** — Strong opening. One sentence communicating business impact. Placeholders for: Hero Image, Role, Timeline, Team, Ownership.
2. **The Opportunity** — Why the product needed to exist. Business challenges, not UX. Clear within one minute.
3. **Looking Beyond Features** — The story of pushing back against complexity; how understanding Excel users changed direction. Communicates product thinking.
4. **Designing the Core Experience** — Largest section. Organize around **4–5 major workflows**, each as: **Challenge → Design Decision → Outcome**. Include visual placeholders. Don't over-describe UI.
5. **Designing for Enterprise Scale** — How validation, permissions, audit history, versioning, dependencies, governance shaped the product. Focus on **why** they matter.
6. **Beyond Design** — Contributions outside UI. Product ownership. Collaboration with PMs, engineers, stakeholders. Make the designer feel like a Product Designer, not a UI Designer.
7. **Impact** — Summarize business outcomes. Tie back to solving operational problems, not shipping features.
8. **Reflection** — One memorable reflection on how this project changed how the designer thinks about enterprise software. End: this person designs **products, not just interfaces.**

## Design Writing Style
When a UI screenshot appears, **write around it** (Challenge / Decision / Result), don't describe it. Assume visuals tell half the story; text explains the thinking.

## What to AVOID
Personas · Empathy Maps · Journey Maps · Competitive Analysis · Design Thinking diagrams · Double Diamond · Wireframe galleries · Large research sections · Long explanations of UX methods.

## Success Criteria
Reader's final thought: *"This designer isn't just creating attractive interfaces. He understands business goals, simplifies technical complexity, influences product strategy, and designs enterprise software that people can confidently adopt."*
